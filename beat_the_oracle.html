This started as my capstone for the Google Data Analytics certificate: a prediction engine built on 125 years of international football results. Every team carries an Elo rating; every match is decided by one probability formula; the whole tournament is simulated thousands of times to estimate who lifts the trophy.

The Oracle always backs the higher-rated side. You don't have to. Predict the real 2026 group stage and knockouts yourself — match it to keep pace, but call an upset it never saw and you bank Defiance points the model can't earn. Out-think the machine.

How it was built

From capstone to game

The data. Four datasets — historical World Cups, every World Cup match, player records, and an Elo rating series spanning 1901–2026. Cleaning and EDA were done in a Jupyter notebook (pandas); this build embeds the cleaned per-team ratings for the real 48 qualifiers and their actual group draw.

The algorithm. Group tables come from a round-robin simulation (Elo win/draw/goal model); the knockout uses single-draw resolution; title odds come from a Monte Carlo over the full bracket. Transparent probability, in homage to Alan Turing — born this June in 1912 — whose Bayesian methods broke Enigma at Bletchley. The Oracle is the machine; you're the human in the loop.

Google AI. Scouting reports are generated per team; in the deployed build the call routes to the Gemini API (see getScoutingReport() — the offline build ships a deterministic fallback so it always runs).
